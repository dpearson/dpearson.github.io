That'll never happen, right?

We've all thought this, at one time or another. It's a fairly easy way to procrastinate; why do something "meaningless?" I've been burned recently, as you might guess. It started in November: a tied football game.

Ties in the NFL are rare: only three have occurred this century. As such, the support for ties in NFLScoreBot was, at best, halfhearted. In English, everything was fine; the summaries weren't particularly elegant, but it worked. As I found that day, I had never actually bothered to do the translation work for French output. I got it working, but I've never implemented a feature faster.

I got burned again this month. The odds of two pieces of information being scored at the exact same value is ridiculously low. It happened, and I found a bug in my code that led to information being associated with the wrong team.

The third time it got me? There have been six double-overtime games in NFL history; one of them was this week. Thankfully, it was a quick fix.

Laziness really does catch up to you. Some of these problems were things I would be unlikely to catch; the second problem came down to a single use of < instead of <=.

The others, though, were things I knew about. I though I could get away with them for just one more season. Then I'd fix it.

I couldn't.

Validation is wonderful (even in really small quantities)

Yesterday, up popped a notification from Tweetbot: NFLScoreBot had been mentioned by a television station in Denver, @KDVR. When I went looking, I saw that they had used one of NFLScoreBot's tweets in their liveblog of the Broncos game earlier that day.

It may sound cheesy, but that realization more-or-less made my day. The project has never gotten very much attention, so even this little tidbit was wonderful to see. If I needed any motivation to stick with the project, that mention, which was probably random, provided it.

Validation, like this event for me, can be simple and small, ranging from a writeup on TechCrunch to a single, solitary new user. It doesn't matter: knowing that someone thought your project valuable makes all the difference; this validation really is the most powerful motivator, even on the smallest of scales.

In the end, I'm likely not one to be offering advice, but I'll do it anyway: stick with your projects, even if they feel like failures. It might just make your day, even if it's not the archetypal success story (which, for all the press these stories get, is uncommon).

The iPad mini's glaring weakness

Over the weekend, I had the opportunity to play with one of the new iPad minis in my local Apple store. While I must profess myself to be impressed, there did nevertheless immediately emerge one significant weakness. Spoiler alert: it's not the display.

Most reviewers have dinged the display quality, but I was actually impressed with it. Granted, I was, at the time, using a first-generation iPad, but I thought the display held up well, especially given the generally-horrible in-store lighting.

Rather, the problem is the device's intended orientation. When you pick the iPad mini up, it feels like a portrait device, and, that way, it is quite comfortable to hold. It certainly supports landscape orientations, though, for some reason, it doesn't feel quite right.

The problem there is that, over the past two and a half years, developers, including Apple, seem to have designed for landscape first. While the built-in Mail app, again, functions in portrait, message listing is normally hidden away in the detail view, creating a minor annoyance (at least for me). On the other hand, MLB At Bat simply does not support portrait orientation on the iPad, forcing the device to be rotated.

Maybe I'm nitpicking. Maybe I'm the only one who sees this as a problem. But the iPad mini's focus on portrait orientation, where it feels best in your hand, is, I think, its only real weakness.
