Addicted to Twitter

I think I'm addicted to Twitter. Why? Every time I'm the least bit bored or feeling too lazy to work, I reach for my iPad to check Twitter. I read literally every tweet that pops up in my timeline over the course of the day. When I open Tweetbot and there are no new tweets, I start looking to follow more people so that I never have that feeling again. Checking Twitter is the first thing I do when I get up in the morning. Every day.

It's not just Twitter, unfortunately. Google+ and App.net are also filling similar roles, although I'm slightly less obsessive-compulsive about them. Still, I wonder how much time I've lost between those three networks over the past year, not to mention other years. My guess? Far, far too much.

I suppose that this behavior is considered acceptable, if not outright encouraged, in some circles. For me, though, it has become and enormous, meaningless time suck. I've completely quit Twitter for short periods of time in the past, and I think it's time to do it again. For one month (to start with), I won't be checking Twitter, save for my (relatively few) mentions. I might post a couple of times, but there will be no more combing through my timeline in a desperate search for something, anything to read.

Hopefully, This break will give me more time to actually do stuff. I have several projects that I've been meaning to work on which I just haven't had enough time for, in part because of Twitter. I'd like to write more here, creating longer pieces that I actually own.

We'll see how it goes. All I know is this: when I've backed away from Twitter in the past, I've felt better. I'm hoping I have that feeling again.

Am I crazy, or is this actually a good idea? Discuss this post on Hacker News.

Ending support for node-identi.ca

A little while ago, the team behind identi.ca announced that they were pulling the plug on the site and moving everything over to pump.io. I'm sure there will be a ton of benefits for identi.ca's users.

The fact is, I'm not one of them. I never really was. I flirted with bringing NFLScoreBot over to identi.ca, which was why I built node-identi.ca to begin with. It was completely the wrong audience, but I had really only done it for fun.

Pump.io will have a very different API, rather than the simple, Twitter-compatible API identi.ca exposed. Therefore, I won't be updating this library to support it; the effort just isn't worth it. I'll leave the code live, as the authorization tool is a decent example of an OAuth flow for node.js.

Software duct tape

Everyone who has written even a few lines of code has heard the mantra: "ship early, ship often." It's not entirely bad advice; sometimes, you do indeed have to make some compromises to get software out the door.

There is, however, an unfortunate tendency when rushing to finish something, especially with a hard deadline looming. I've taken to calling it (after a physical analogue) "duct tape." In it nutshell, duct taped software is anything you're afraid to touch, lest something break. It defies refactoring attempts, and it has tremendous staying power.

My cringeworthy example? NHLScoreBot. It was completely rewritten this spring to finally fix an awful mistake: initially, it was a complete fork of the codebase for the football versions.

That worked great for a while. There was a lot of shared code around summary generation, and I had to quickly ship to present in a competition. Everything mostly worked, too; I had spent a bit of time chasing down all of the football-specific code. As long as I didn't breathe too loudly, it churned out decent summaries.

Eventually, though, I had had enough. I spent a couple of weeks worth of free time extracting all of that shared summary-generation code into its own library. There were still sport-specific changes, but those are now miniscule. I got a lot of functionality for free, without difficult merges every time something minor changed. In short, it's what I probably should have done initially.

But, looking back, it's hard to fault the duct tape too much. The horribly-rigged mashup of code specific to two completely different sports allowed me to ship quickly and bought me time for a difficult refactor. That refactor, too, was probably inevitable, and this one messy project taught me that lesson.

Hopefully, it's a lesson that I won't have to relearn.
